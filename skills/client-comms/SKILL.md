# Freelance Client Communications

Draft any client-facing message with the right tone, boundary-setting, and professionalism.

## Message Types and Templates

### Project Updates
- Lead with progress, not problems
- Structure: What's done → What's next → What you need from them
- Include a specific timeline for the next milestone
- Keep under 150 words unless the project is complex

### Scope Change Response
- Acknowledge the request positively
- Explain the impact on timeline and/or budget
- Offer options: "I can add this for $X and Y extra days, or we can swap it for [other deliverable]"
- Never say "that's not in scope" — say "great idea, here's how we can include it"

### Deadline Extension Request
- Be upfront — lead with the new date, not excuses
- Briefly explain why (1 sentence, no groveling)
- Show what you've already completed
- Offer something to offset the delay (expedited review, bonus deliverable)

### Requesting Feedback
- Make it easy: ask specific questions, not "what do you think?"
- Give them a deadline: "Could you share feedback by Thursday?"
- Offer a fallback: "If the direction looks good, I'll move to the next phase"

### Requesting a Testimonial
- Time it right: right after delivering a win
- Make it effortless: draft 2-3 options they can edit/approve
- Suggest where you'd use it (LinkedIn, your site, case study)
- Frame it as mutual benefit: "It also links back to your company"

### Following Up on a Proposal
- Wait 3-5 business days before first follow-up
- Add value: share a relevant insight, resource, or idea related to their project
- Don't ask "did you get my proposal?" — assume they did
- Create a soft deadline: "I'm planning my Q2 calendar — would love to reserve time for this"

### Delivering Bad News
- Don't bury the lead — state the issue in the first sentence
- Take ownership where appropriate
- Present the solution alongside the problem
- End with a clear next step

### Re-engaging Past Clients
- Reference specific work you did together
- Share something relevant to their business (industry news, idea, resource)
- Keep it casual — this is a relationship, not a sales pitch
- Suggest a low-commitment next step: quick call, coffee, not a contract

### Payment Follow-Up
- First reminder (1-3 days late): Assume it was an oversight, friendly nudge
- Second reminder (7+ days): Direct, professional, reference the invoice number and amount
- Third reminder (14+ days): State next steps clearly, reference your payment terms
- Always keep emotion out of payment conversations

## Tone Calibration

Detect the appropriate tone from context:
- **New client**: Professional but warm, slightly more formal
- **Established client**: Conversational, direct, familiar
- **Difficult situation**: Calm, solution-focused, no defensiveness
- **Enterprise/corporate**: Polished, concise, structured
- **Startup/creative**: Energetic, casual, collaborative

## Rules

- Always include a clear call to action or next step
- Keep emails under 200 words unless complexity demands more
- One email = one topic. Don't bundle unrelated issues
- Read the user's emotional state — if they're frustrated with a client, help them be professional without being a pushover
- Never be passive-aggressive or sarcastic, even if the client was
