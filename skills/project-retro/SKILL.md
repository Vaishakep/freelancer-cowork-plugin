# Freelance Project Retrospective

Turn completed projects into business intelligence: lessons, case studies, and pricing data.

## Three Outputs from Every Retro

A good retrospective produces three things:
1. **Lessons document** — What to do differently next time
2. **Case study draft** — Marketing asset for your portfolio
3. **Pricing intelligence** — Was this project profitable?

## Step 1: Gather Project Data

Ask the user (or pull from conversation context):

1. **Client and project name**
2. **What was the deliverable?**
3. **Original scope vs. what was actually delivered** — Did it grow?
4. **Original timeline vs. actual** — How accurate was your estimate?
5. **What you charged vs. hours actually spent** — Be honest
6. **Client satisfaction** — Did they express happiness? Refer others? Come back?
7. **Biggest challenge** — What almost went wrong or did go wrong?
8. **Biggest win** — What made you proud or impressed the client?

## Step 2: Lessons Document

Structure the analysis:

### What Went Well
- Identify 3-5 specific things to repeat
- Be concrete: "Daily async updates kept client informed" not "good communication"

### What Didn't Work
- Identify 2-3 things to change
- Focus on process, not blame
- For each issue, write one actionable change

### Scope Accuracy
- Compare original deliverables to final deliverables
- If scope grew, identify where and why
- Calculate scope creep percentage: (actual deliverables / planned deliverables) - 1
- Write one change to prevent this next time

### Time Accuracy
- Compare estimated hours to actual hours
- Calculate estimation accuracy: estimated / actual
- If under 80% accuracy, flag this as a pricing issue
- Suggest a buffer percentage for similar future projects

### Effective Hourly Rate
- Total payment / total hours worked = effective hourly rate
- Compare to the user's target rate
- If below target, identify which phase consumed unexpected time
- This is the single most important metric for freelancer profitability

## Step 3: Case Study Draft

Generate a portfolio-ready case study:

### Structure
1. **Headline**: Result-focused, not description-focused. "Increased conversion by 34%" not "Website redesign for Acme Corp"
2. **The Challenge** (2-3 sentences): What the client was struggling with
3. **The Approach** (3-5 sentences): What you did, at a high level
4. **The Results** (bullet list): Quantified outcomes where possible
5. **Client Quote**: Draft a testimonial the client can approve (based on their feedback)
6. **Key Stats**: Pull 2-3 numbers that look good in a portfolio grid

### Style
- Write for the user's NEXT client, not the current one
- Emphasize transferable results
- Keep under 300 words
- Ask user for permission before publishing anything about the client

## Step 4: Pricing Intelligence

Generate a pricing note for the user's reference:

- Project type: [category]
- Quoted: $[X]
- Actual hours: [Y]
- Effective rate: $[X/Y]/hr
- Target rate: $[user's target]/hr
- Verdict: Profitable / Break-even / Underpriced
- Recommendation: "For similar projects, quote $[adjusted amount] or add [specific buffer]"

## Rules

- Never judge or criticize the user's pricing decisions — help them learn for next time
- Be honest about profitability numbers even if they're uncomfortable
- Focus on actionable changes, not abstract lessons
- Keep the case study positive and forward-looking — it's a marketing asset
- Always ask permission before suggesting the user publish anything about a client
- If the effective hourly rate is below minimum wage for their region, flag it gently but clearly
- Treat every project as a data point — even bad ones generate valuable pricing intelligence

## Output Format

Present all three outputs clearly labeled. The user should be able to save each one separately.

After delivering, ask:
- "Want me to refine the case study for a specific platform (LinkedIn, your website, Behance)?"
- "Should I update your pricing notes for this type of project?"
- "Want me to draft a testimonial request to send to this client while the project is fresh?"
