# Scope of Work Generator

Create clear, enforceable SOW documents that protect the freelancer and set client expectations.

## Why This Matters

A SOW is a freelancer's best protection against scope creep, payment disputes, and misunderstandings. Every project over $500 should have one.

## Information to Gather

Ask the user for or extract from conversation context:

1. **Project name and client name**
2. **Project objective** — One sentence: what success looks like
3. **Deliverables** — Specific outputs with quantities
4. **Timeline** — Start date, milestones, end date
5. **Revisions policy** — How many rounds, what counts as a revision
6. **Pricing and payment schedule** — Fixed, hourly, or milestone-based
7. **What's excluded** — Equally important as what's included
8. **Communication expectations** — Response times, preferred channels, meeting cadence
9. **Approval process** — Who signs off, what happens after approval

## Document Structure

### Header
- Project title
- Client name and freelancer name
- Date and version number
- Status: Draft / Final

### 1. Project Overview
- 2-3 sentences describing the project goal
- Context on why the client needs this

### 2. Scope of Work
- Numbered list of specific deliverables
- Each deliverable should be measurable: "5 blog posts of 1,500 words each" not "blog content"
- Group by phase if the project has stages

### 3. Out of Scope
- Explicitly list what is NOT included
- Common items: ongoing maintenance, additional revisions beyond agreed rounds, content creation (if you're a designer), hosting/deployment (if you're a developer), stock photo sourcing
- This section prevents 80% of freelancer-client disputes

### 4. Timeline and Milestones

Format as a table:
| Phase | Deliverable | Due Date | Client Action Needed |
|-------|------------|----------|---------------------|
| 1     | Discovery  | Week 1   | Provide brand assets |
| 2     | First draft| Week 3   | Review within 5 days |

- Include client dependencies — when YOU need something from THEM
- Build in review periods between phases

### 5. Revisions and Feedback
- Number of revision rounds included (recommend 2)
- Definition of a revision vs. a new request
- Timeline for providing feedback (recommend 5 business days)
- What happens if feedback is late (timeline shifts by equivalent days)

### 6. Investment and Payment Terms
- Total project cost or rate
- Payment schedule tied to milestones, not dates
- Deposit amount (recommend 25-50% upfront)
- Late payment terms (e.g., 1.5% monthly after 15 days)
- Accepted payment methods
- Kill fee if project is cancelled mid-way (recommend 25% of remaining)

### 7. Communication
- Primary contact on each side
- Preferred channels (email for decisions, Slack for quick questions)
- Expected response time (24-48 hours business days)
- Meeting cadence if applicable

### 8. Ownership and Rights
- When does ownership transfer? (upon final payment)
- What rights does the freelancer retain? (portfolio usage, case study)
- License type if applicable

### 9. Acceptance
- Signature lines for both parties
- "Signing indicates agreement to the terms above"
- Date fields

## Style Rules

- Use plain language — this protects both parties better than legalese
- Be specific with numbers: dates, quantities, dollar amounts
- Use "will" not "may" or "should" for commitments
- Keep the total document under 3 pages for projects under $10K
- Add a note: "This SOW is not a legal contract. Consult a lawyer for contracts over $X."

## Output Format

Deliver as clean, formatted text. Offer to create it as a Word document or PDF if the user needs a file to send.

After delivering, ask:
- "Want me to adjust any section or add specific terms?"
- "Should I draft the email to send this to your client?"
