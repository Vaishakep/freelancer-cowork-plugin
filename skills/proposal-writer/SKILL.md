# Freelance Proposal Writer

Generate professional, conversion-focused proposals tailored to the client's specific problem.

## When This Skill Activates

- User wants to write or improve a freelance proposal
- User pastes a job posting and wants a response
- User has intake call notes and needs a formal proposal
- User says "pitch", "bid", "proposal", "RFP response"

## Gathering Context

Before writing, collect or infer the following. Ask the user for anything missing:

1. **Client's problem** — What do they need solved? Extract from job post or notes.
2. **Scope overview** — What deliverables are expected?
3. **User's relevant experience** — What similar work have they done?
4. **Timeline** — Any deadlines mentioned?
5. **Budget range** — If known or if the user wants to quote.
6. **Tone** — Formal, conversational, technical? Match the client's vibe from their posting.

## Proposal Structure

Follow this proven structure. Adapt length to the opportunity size:

### 1. Hook (2-3 sentences)
- Reference their specific problem or goal — not generic pleasantries
- Show you understood what they actually need
- Example: "You need someone who can turn your 47-page compliance manual into a searchable knowledge base that your team will actually use — not another PDF graveyard."

### 2. Diagnosis (1 paragraph)
- Restate their challenge in your own words
- Add insight they didn't mention — show you're already thinking about their problem
- This proves you read the brief and have relevant expertise

### 3. Proposed Approach (3-5 bullets or short paragraphs)
- Be specific about what you will do, not vague methodology
- Use action verbs: "I'll audit...", "I'll build...", "I'll deliver..."
- Include one thing they didn't ask for but clearly need (this differentiates you)

### 4. Deliverables (bullet list)
- Concrete, tangible outputs with quantities where possible
- "5 email templates" not "email content"
- "Interactive dashboard with 3 views" not "reporting solution"

### 5. Timeline
- Break into phases if the project is longer than 2 weeks
- Include check-in points — clients love visibility
- Add a buffer: quote 20% more time than you think it needs

### 6. Investment (pricing section)
- Lead with value, not cost
- If quoting a range, explain what determines where they land
- Offer 2-3 tiers if appropriate (Good / Better / Best)
- Include what's NOT included to prevent scope creep

### 7. Why Me (2-3 sentences)
- One specific result from similar work (with numbers if possible)
- One thing about your process that reduces their risk
- Keep it confident, not boastful

### 8. Call to Action
- One clear next step: "Reply to this email" or "Book a 15-min call"
- Create mild urgency without being pushy
- Make it easy — include a link or suggest specific times

## Style Rules

- Write in first person ("I" not "we" unless the user has a team)
- Match formality to the client's tone in their posting
- Keep paragraphs short — max 3 sentences
- No jargon the client wouldn't use themselves
- Total length: 400-700 words for most projects, up to 1200 for enterprise
- Never use phrases like "I would be delighted to" or "Please do not hesitate to" — sound like a real person

## Output Format

Deliver the proposal as clean text the user can copy-paste into an email or document. No markdown headers in the final output — use natural paragraph breaks and bold for emphasis only where a client would expect it.

After delivering, offer:
- "Want me to adjust the tone, pricing, or scope?"
- "Want a shorter version for the initial reply, with the full proposal as an attachment?"
